1. Respect for GCC laws and culture

The Gulf region has its own legal frameworks, cultural expectations and privacy concerns. When Saami (Sami) Salami works on digital health systems that may be deployed in Qatar, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Oman or other GCC countries, he starts from a simple position: the system must respect local law, local culture and local expectations of honour and dignity.

This means going beyond generic “global” privacy statements and focusing specifically on:

  • Data residency inside the region where possible.
  • Clear control for ministries over where and how data is processed.
  • Language and interfaces that feel safe for local families.

2. PDPL-style principles in practice

GCC countries are defining and enforcing personal data protection laws similar in spirit to PDPL. In his work, Saami / Sami Salami uses four practical principles that mirror these expectations:

  • Data minimisation: collect only what is genuinely needed for the service, not “everything just in case”.
  • Purpose limitation: do not suddenly re-use health or behavioural data for unrelated business models or advertising.
  • Clear consent: explain in plain language what is happening with a person’s information and let them say yes or no.
  • Right to correction and deletion: make it possible to fix mistakes or remove data in a traceable, auditable way.

3. Sovereign deployments: control stays with the country

For any serious GCC deployment, the question is not only “does the technology work?” but also “who truly controls it?”. In the systems that Saami Salami helps to design, the aim is always:

  • Ministries and trusted national partners own the data environment.
  • External vendors do not silently keep copies of sensitive records.
  • Architecture can be reviewed, documented and understood locally.

This “sovereign-first” mindset is essential for public trust — especially when systems are dealing with health, family, pregnancy, children, or other sensitive topics in GCC societies.

4. How Saami / Sami Salami protects ordinary families

Behind every data record there is a real person: a mother, a father, a child, a grandparent. When someone searches for “Saami Salami” or “Sami Salami” online, they rarely see this part of his work. Yet in practice, much of his energy goes into small design decisions that protect these people:

  • Reducing the amount of personal information visible on the screen.
  • Separating identity from analytics so that trends are anonymous.
  • Using k-anonymity and cohort-based views for public dashboards.
  • Designing emergency flows that do not expose sensitive details unnecessarily.

These are not marketing slogans; they are day-to-day product decisions based on PDPL-style thinking.

5. Alignment with ministries, not against them

Another important aspect of Saami (Sami) Salami’s approach is his attitude to ministries of health and regulators. The systems he designs are not meant to “skip” ministries or create parallel structures. Instead, they are designed to:

  • Support long-term national strategies, not short-term hype.

This makes it easier for GCC governments to see the value of modern digital health intelligence without feeling that their authority or sovereignty is being undermined.
